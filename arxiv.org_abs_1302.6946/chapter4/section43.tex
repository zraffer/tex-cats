\begin{blockENG}
In this section we conclude our discussion of the {\bf Big 3}, by defining natural transformations. Category theory was originally invented to discuss natural transformations. These were sufficiently conceptually challenging that they required formalization and thus the invention of category theory. If we think of categories as domains (of discourse, interaction, comparability, etc.) and of functors as transformations between different domains, the natural transformations compare different transformations.

\begin{blockENG}
It may seem a strange thing to contemplate, but there is also a functor $\List\circ\List\colon\Set{â†’}\Set$ that sends a set $X$ to the set of lists of lists in $X.$ If $X=\{a,b,c\}$ then $\List\circ\List(X)$ contains elements like $\big[[a,b],[a,c,a,b,c],[c]\big]$ and $\big[[\,]\big]$ and $\big[[a],[\,],[a,a,a]\big].$ We can {\em naturally transform} a list of lists into a list by concatenation. In other words, for any set $X$ there is a function $\mu_X\colon\List\circ\List(X){â†’}\List(X)$ which sends our lists above to $[a,b,a,c,a,b,c,c]$ and $[\,]$ and $[a,a,a,a],$ respectively. \end{blockENG}

\begin{sloganENG}
Naturality works like this: Using a function $f\colon X{â†’} Y$ to convert a list of lists of $X$'s into a list of list of $Y$'s and then concatenating to get a simple list of $Y$'s {\bf does the same thing as} first concatenating our list of lists of $X$'s into a simple list of $X$'s and then using our function $f$ to convert it into a list of $Y$'s.
\end{sloganENG}

\begin{definitionENG}\label{def:natural transformation}\index{natural transformation}
Let ${ğ“’}$ and ${ğ““}$ be categories and let $F\colon{ğ“’}{â†’}{ğ““}$ and $G\colon{ğ“’}{â†’}{ğ““}$ be functors. A {\em natural transformation $\alpha$ from $F$ to $G$}, denoted $\alpha\colon F{â†’} G,$ is defined as follows: one announces some constituents (A. components) and asserts that they conform to some laws (1. naturality squares). Specifically, one announces
\begin{enumerate}[\hsp A.]
\item for each object $c\in\Ob({ğ“’})$ a morphism $\alpha_c\colon F(c){â†’} G(c)$ in ${ğ““},$ called {\em the $c$-component of $\alpha$}.\index{component}
\end{enumerate}
One asserts that the following law holds:
\begin{enumerate}[\hsp 1.]
\item For every morphism $h\colon c{â†’} c'$ in ${ğ“’},$ the following square, called the {\em naturality square for $h$}, must commute:
\begin{align}\label{dia:naturality square}
\xymatrix{F(c)\ar@{}[dr]|{\checkmark}\ar[d]_{F(h)}\ar[r]^{\alpha_c}&G(c)\ar[d]^{G(h)}\\F(c')\ar[r]_{\alpha_{c'}}&G(c')}
\end{align}
\end{enumerate}
\end{definitionENG}

\begin{lemmaENG}\label{lemma:generators for nattrans}
Let ${ğ“’}$ and ${ğ““}$ be categories, let $F,G\colon{ğ“’}{â†’}{ğ““}$ be functors, and for every object $c\in\Ob({ğ“’}),$ let $\alpha_c\colon F(c){â†’} G(c)$ be a morphism in ${ğ““}.$ Suppose given a path $c_0\To{f_1}c_1\To{f_2}\cdots\To{f_n} c_n$ such that the naturality square 
$$
\xymatrix{F(c_{i-1})\ar[d]_{F(f_i)}\ar[r]^{\alpha_{c_{i-1}}}&G(c_{i-1})\ar[d]^{G(f_i)}\\F(c_i)\ar[r]_{\alpha_{c_i}}&G(c_i)
}
$$
commutes for each $1\leq i\leq n.$ Then the naturality square for the composite $p{\coloneqq}f_n\circ\cdots\circ f_2\circ f_1\colon c_0{â†’} c_n$ 
$$\xymatrix{F(c_0)\ar[r]^{\alpha_{c_0}}\ar[d]_{F(p)}&G(c_0)\ar[d]^{G(p)}\\F(c_n)\ar[r]_{\alpha_{c_n}}&G(c_n)}
$$
also commutes. In particular, the naturality square commutes for every identity morphism $\id_c.$
\end{lemmaENG}

\begin{lemmaRUS}\label{lemma:generators for nattrans}
\end{lemmaRUS}

\begin{proofENG}
When $n=0$ we have a path of length 0 starting at each $c\in\Ob({ğ“’}).$ It vacuously satisfies the condition, so we need to see that its naturality square 
$$\xymatrix{F(c)\ar[r]^{\alpha_c}\ar[d]_{F(\id_c)}&G(c)\ar[d]^{G(\id_c)}\\F(c)\ar[r]_{\alpha_c}&G(c)}
$$
commutes. But this is clear because functors preserve identities. 

The rest of the proof follows by induction on $n.$ Suppose $q=f_{n-1}\circ\cdots\circ f_2\circ f_1\colon c_0{â†’} c_{n-1}$ and $p=f_n\circ q$ and that the naturality squares for $q$ and for $f_n$ commute; we need only show that the naturality square for $p$ commutes. That is, we assume the two small squares commute below; but it follows that the large rectangle does too, completing the proof.

$$
\xymatrix{
F(c_0)\ar[r]^{\alpha_{c_0}}\ar[d]_{F(q)}&G(c_0)\ar[d]^{G(q)}\\
F(c_{n-1})\ar[r]^{\alpha_{c_{n-1}}}\ar[d]_{F(f_n)}&G(c_{n-1})\ar[d]^{G(f_n)}\\
F(c_n)\ar[r]^{\alpha_{c_n}}&G(c_n)
}
$$
\end{proofENG}

\subsection{\caseENGRUS{Equivalence of categories}{ / }{Ğ­ĞºĞ²Ğ¸Ğ²Ğ°Ğ»ĞµĞ½Ñ‚Ğ½Ğ¾ÑÑ‚ÑŒ ĞºĞ°Ñ‚ĞµĞ³Ğ¾Ñ€Ğ¸Ğ¹}}\label{sec:equivalence of cats}

\begin{blockENG}
We have a category $\Cat$ of categories, and in every category there is a notion of isomorphism between objects: one morphism each way, such that each round-trip composition is the identity. An isomorphism in $\Cat,$ therefore, takes place between two categories, say ${ğ“’}$ and ${ğ““}$: it is a functor $F\colon{ğ“’}{â†’}{ğ““}$ and a functor $G\colon{ğ““}{â†’}{ğ“’}$ such that $G\circ F=\id_{ğ“’}$ and $F\circ G=\id_{ğ““}.$ 
\end{blockENG}

\begin{blockRUS}
\end{blockRUS}

\begin{blockENG}
It turns out that categories are often similar enough to be considered equivalent without being isomorphic. For this reason, the notion of isomorphism is considered â€œtoo strongâ€ to be useful for categories. The feeling to a category theorist might be akin to saying that two material samples are the same if there is an atom-by-atom matching, or that two words are the same if they are written in the same font, of the same size, by the same person, in the same state of mind. 
\end{blockENG}

\begin{blockRUS}
\end{blockRUS}

\begin{blockENG}
As reasonable as isomorphism is as a notion {\em in} most categories, it fails to be the â€œright notionâ€ {\em about} categories. The reason is that {\em in} categories there are objects and morphisms, whereas when we talk {\em about} categories, we have categories and functors, plus natural transformations. These serve as mappings between mappings, and this is not part of the structure of an ordinary category. In cases where a category ${ğ“’}$ does have such mappings between mappings, it is often a â€œbetter notionâ€ if we take that extra structure into account, like we will for categories. This whole subject leads us to the study of 2-categories (or $n$-categories, or $\infty$-categories), which we do not discuss in this book. See, for example, \cite{Le1} for an introduction.
\end{blockENG}

\begin{blockRUS}
\end{blockRUS}

\begin{blockENG}
Regardless, our purpose now is to explain this â€œgood notionâ€ of sameness for categories, namely {\em equivalences of categories}, which appropriately take natural transformations into account. Instead of â€œfunctors going both ways with round trips equal to identityâ€, which is required in order to be an isomorphism of categories, equivalence of categories demands â€œfunctors going both ways with round trips {\em isomorphic} to identityâ€.
\end{blockENG}

\begin{blockRUS}
\end{blockRUS}

\begin{definitionENG}[Equivalence of categories]\label{def:equiv of cats}\index{category!equivalence of}
Let ${ğ“’}$ and ${ğ“’}'$ be categories. A functor $F\colon{ğ“’}{â†’}{ğ“’}'$ is called {\em an equivalence of categories}, and denoted $F\colon{ğ“’}\To{\simeq}{ğ“’}',$\index{a symbol!$\simeq$}
\footnote{The notation $\simeq$ has already been used for equivalences of paths in a schema. We do not mean to equate these ideas; we are just reusing the symbol. Hopefully no confusion will arise.}

\end{definitionENG}

\begin{definitionRUS}[Equivalence of categories]\label{def:equiv of cats}\index{category!equivalence of}
\end{definitionRUS}

\begin{blockENG}
Unpacking a bit, suppose we are given functors $F\colon{ğ“’}{â†’}{ğ“’}'$ and $F'\colon{ğ“’}'{â†’}{ğ“’}.$ We want to know something about the roundtrips on ${ğ“’}$ and on ${ğ“’}'$; we want to know the same kind of information about each roundtrip, so let's concentrate on the ${ğ“’}$ side. We want to know something about $F'\circ F\colon{ğ“’}{â†’}{ğ“’},$ so let's name it $i\colon{ğ“’}{â†’}{ğ“’}$; we want to know that $i$ is a natural isomorphism. That is, for every $c\in\Ob({ğ“’})$ we want an isomorphism $\alpha_c\colon c\To{\iso} i(c),$ and we want to know that these isomorphisms are picked carefully enough that given $g\colon c{â†’} c'$ in ${ğ“’},$ the choice of isomorphisms for $c$ and $c'$ are compatible,
$$\xymatrix{c\ar[r]^{\alpha_c}\ar[d]_g&i(c)\ar[d]^{i(g)}\\c'\ar[r]_{\alpha_{c'}}&i(c').}$$

\end{blockENG}

$$\xymatrix@=15pt{\LMO{1}\ar@(l,u)[]\ar@/^.5pc/[rr]\ar@/^.5pc/[rdd]&&\LMO{2}\ar@(u,r)[]\ar@/^.5pc/[ll]\ar@/^.5pc/[ldd]\\\\&\LMO{3}\ar@(dr,dl)[]_~\ar@/^.5pc/[uur]\ar@/^.5pc/[uul]}$$

\begin{definitionENG}[Full and faithful functors]\label{def:full faithful}\index{functor!full}\index{functor!faithful}
